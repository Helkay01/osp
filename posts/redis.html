Project Spotlight: Redis — What Its License Reversal Says About Trust

6 min read
← Back to all posts

Redis was created by Salvatore Sanfilippo (widely known as "antirez") in 2009 as a fast, in-memory key-value data store, and it grew into one of the most widely deployed caching and data-structure servers in the industry, historically distributed under the permissive BSD-3-Clause license.

In March 2024, Redis Ltd. changed the license on new versions of Redis to a dual-license model combining the Server Side Public License (SSPL) and the Redis Source Available License v2 (RSALv2) — neither of which is recognized as open source by the Open Source Initiative. The company framed the change as a response to large cloud providers offering managed Redis services without contributing back.

The reaction from parts of the community was sharp enough that a group of maintainers and companies forked the last BSD-licensed version to create Valkey, placed under the neutral governance of the Linux Foundation.

In May 2025, Redis reversed course, adding the OSI-approved AGPLv3 as a licensing option starting with Redis 8 — a move the company described directly as returning to open source. Sanfilippo, who had rejoined Redis as a developer advocate in late 2024, publicly welcomed the change. The episode is now frequently cited in open source governance discussions as an example of how quickly a relicensing decision can fracture a community, and how a fork's momentum can pressure a company to reconsider.
