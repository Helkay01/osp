Project Spotlight: Kubernetes — The Container Orchestrator That Became the Default

6 min read
← Back to all posts

Kubernetes was developed at Google, drawing on lessons from Borg, the internal cluster management system Google had used for years to run its own infrastructure. Google open-sourced Kubernetes in 2014, and its name — Greek for "helmsman" or "pilot" — reflects its role steering containerized workloads across a cluster of machines.

Rather than keeping Kubernetes under its own control, Google donated the project to the newly formed Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF) in 2015, placing it under vendor-neutral governance. That decision is widely credited with accelerating adoption, since competing cloud providers and companies could contribute and rely on Kubernetes without worrying it was steering exclusively toward Google's commercial interests.

The project automates the deployment, scaling, and management of containerized applications, handling tasks like load balancing, self-healing when containers fail, and rolling out updates without downtime — problems that previously required significant custom tooling to solve well.

Kubernetes is released under the Apache 2.0 license, and its ecosystem has grown to include a large number of CNCF-hosted companion projects covering networking, storage, observability, and security, making "Kubernetes-native" a common design goal for new infrastructure tooling.
