Project Spotlight: Godot Engine — A Community-Owned Alternative in Game Development

5 min read
← Back to all posts

Godot began as an in-house engine built by Argentine developers Juan Linietsky and Ariel Manzur for their own studio projects, before being open-sourced in 2014. Its scene-and-node architecture and built-in scripting language (GDScript) were designed to make game structure easy to reason about, rather than bolting scripting onto an engine built for something else.

Godot is released under the MIT license, one of the most permissive options available, with no royalty or revenue-share requirements — a deliberate contrast to commercial engines that take a percentage of a shipped game's revenue above certain thresholds.

The project is stewarded by the nonprofit Godot Foundation, which manages funding and infrastructure while development itself is driven by a large volunteer contributor base alongside a smaller group of core maintainers.

Interest in Godot has grown alongside broader industry debate over engine licensing terms, with a number of studios and hobbyist developers evaluating it as an alternative to Unity and Unreal Engine for both 2D and 3D projects.
