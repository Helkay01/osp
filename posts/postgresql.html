Project Spotlight: PostgreSQL — The Database Community Ownership Kept Independent

5 min read
← Back to all posts

PostgreSQL traces back to POSTGRES, a research database project led by Michael Stonebraker at the University of California, Berkeley, in the 1980s. After the university-funded research wound down, outside developers picked up the codebase, added SQL support, and released it under a permissive license — the lineage that became the PostgreSQL known today.

That community-first origin has shaped how the project is run ever since. There is no single company that owns PostgreSQL or controls its roadmap. Development is coordinated by a core team and a much larger group of contributors, with releases decided by rough consensus rather than a corporate release calendar. Several companies sell PostgreSQL-based products and cloud services, but none of them control the upstream project.

That independence is often cited as a reason large organizations choose PostgreSQL over vendor-controlled alternatives: the roadmap isn't tied to any one company's commercial incentives, and the permissive PostgreSQL License imposes very few restrictions on how the software is used or redistributed.

Today PostgreSQL is used everywhere from small applications to large-scale production systems, and it continues to ship a major version roughly once a year, with contributions coming from individual developers and multiple competing companies alike.
