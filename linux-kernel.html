Project Spotlight: The Linux Kernel — Three Decades of Collaborative Engineering

5 min read
← Back to all posts

Linus Torvalds began writing the Linux kernel in 1991 as a personal project while a student at the University of Helsinki, initially announcing it in a Usenet post as "just a hobby, won't be big and professional." Paired with the GNU tools already being developed by the Free Software Foundation, it formed the basis of a complete free operating system.

The kernel is released under the GPLv2 license, which requires that modifications distributed to others be released under the same terms — a licensing choice that has remained a point of ongoing discussion as the kernel has become commercially significant to companies well beyond its original hobbyist origins.

Torvalds still serves as the project's lead maintainer, and development is organized through a hierarchy of subsystem maintainers who review and merge changes into their areas before those changes flow up into mainline releases, coordinated through the Linux Foundation.

Today the kernel underpins the large majority of the world's top supercomputers, the Android operating system on billions of mobile devices, and a substantial share of servers and cloud infrastructure — an unusually broad footprint for a single codebase with a single named lead maintainer three decades after it started.
